Traffic Stops

Stanford University's Open Policing Project has collected and standardized over 130 million traffic stop records from 31 state police agencies. This data includes information such as stop date, reason, and location, which we've begun exploring below.

There are more than 50,000 traffic stops on a typical day in the U.S. A plurality of these stops take place on Friday (~16.1%), with speeding violations accounting for nearly half (~49%) of all stops.

Use-of-Force (UoF) Incidents

Definition

Use of Force is the means of compelling compliance or overcoming resistance to an officer's efforts to protect life or property, or take a person into custody. [1]

OpenPDI has standardized over 50,000 UoF incident reports from data submitted to the Police Data Initiative. This gives us the opportunity to explore why and how police officers are applying the Use of Force across the U.S.

33,385 of these reports specified a reason for using force, as shown in the table below.

Reasons for using force (33,385 incidents)
Reason Example Column Entries Percent of Incidents
Resistance "resisting arrest", "necessary to effect arrest" 40.9
Active Aggression "assaulting citizen(s)", "subject combative", "battery on police officer" 27.2
Non-compliance "refuse verbal commands", "subject non compliant" 11.5
Fleeing "fleeing", "flight from an officer", "escape" 5.2
Weapon Display "weapon display", "weapon exhibited" 3.9
Other "other", "to restrain for subjects safety", "in custody, maintaining control" 11.3

Officers are typically trained to use the least amount of force necessary to "reduce the immediate threat," ranging from No Force (verbal commands, officer presence, etc.) to Deadly Force (their firearm).
